COMMUNITY

At aSuggestion, we encourage every author to write with clarity, respect, and consideration. This is not a space for conflict, but rather a community for growth, understanding, and meaningful connection.

When you encounter perspectives that differ from your own, see them as opportunities—to listen, to share, to learn, and perhaps even to teach. We remind our readers to pause before reacting, reflect on the comments received, and respond thoughtfully. Disagreement is natural, but retaliation is not the answer. Individual dealing with IDD

IDD stands for Intellectual and Developmental Disabilities, which means you might learn or do some things differently from others — and that's completely okay. Everyone grows and develops at their own pace, and having IDD simply means you may need a bit more support in certain areas of life such as learning, communicating, or completing everyday tasks.

Taking care of yourself is important, and there are many simple ways to do it. Following a daily routine can make life feel easier and less stressful, while eating healthy foods and staying active helps you feel better overall. Try doing tasks on your own when possible, but remember it's always okay to ask for help. Regular visits to doctors and therapists are important for your health, and spending time with friends, family, and supportive people helps you stay connected.

With patience and practice, you can become more confident in your abilities. It's important to focus on your strengths and use them to overcome challenges. Remember that everyone needs support sometimes, and asking for it shows courage. Surround yourself with positive people who believe in you and encourage your growth. Each step you take, no matter how small, brings you closer to living a happier and more independent life.

Families

Having a loved one with an Intellectual and Developmental Disability (IDD) can bring unique challenges — and also deep joy and meaning. Every person with IDD is different, and every family's journey is unique. One of the most helpful things you can do is create a consistent routine, since daily structure helps reduce anxiety and supports independence.

Caring for overall well-being is just as important. Helping with balanced meals, regular physical activity, good sleep, and mental health care lays the foundation for long-term health. Keeping up with medical and therapy appointments — whether speech, occupational, or behavioral therapies — also plays a key role in ongoing development.

It's equally essential to focus on strengths rather than just needs. Celebrating your loved one's abilities and interests nurtures their sense of purpose and shows the many ways they can contribute. Building a strong support network is also crucial, as no family should feel they have to face the journey alone.

Caregiver

As a caregiver for someone with an Intellectual and Developmental Disability (IDD), your role is both deeply important and uniquely demanding. It can bring moments of reward, joy, and connection, but it can also feel challenging and, at times, overwhelming. Establishing a consistent routine is one of the most effective ways to create structure, reduce stress, and help your loved one feel safe and confident.

Supporting healthy living is another essential part of caregiving. This includes helping with nutritious meals, encouraging regular physical activity, promoting good sleep, and supporting emotional well-being. Staying on top of medical and therapy care is also crucial — from keeping track of appointments and medications to noticing any changes in behavior or health. Clear and accessible communication plays a key role as well, whether through visuals, gestures, simple words, or assistive devices. Practicing patience and empathy throughout the journey ensures that progress, however gradual, is met with encouragement rather than judgment.
